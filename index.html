Investigating Trends In The New York Times Through Visualization

By Ethan Brooks and Tamur Asar

The following visualizations were constructed using a collection of over two million New York Times articles. The visualizations aim to illustrate common pattens and trends, be that cultural or semantic, that subliminaly exist and/or are intentionally constructed by The New York Times. While we do not have comprehensive datasets for other news agencies to compare to, we aim to extrapolate our findings as an explanation to broader trends that morph news during different time periods.

Which New York Times sections produce the most news?

The visualation above, called a Tree Map, shows the breakdown of published articles by section for a given year. We decided to use a Tree Map because it is effect at conveying how indiviudal sections contribute to the total volume of published articles. By interacting with the year slider, users can explore trends over time. For example, they might discover growth in technology-related articles with the rise of the internet and digital culture. They might also discover the New York Section was common during the post 9/11 period, that the Business Day section rose during the 2007 financial crisis, and that the World section rose during 2003 Iraq War. Viewers may also notice that some sections hold a relatively constant percent makeup of total articles published, while others fluctuate greatly year to year.

What were the the most common words of each year?

"Hot topic" words can give us insights into the current events within a given year. The visualization above shows for each year the top ten largest growing keywords from the previous year. We decided on a word cloud because it allows users to visualize the growth of top keywords and compare them relative to others. The size of each word indicates the magnitude of growth, making it easy to identify which topics have gained the most traction. As one might expect, in 2001, the keywords 'New York City' and 'Terrorism' grew significantly from the previous year, reflecting the impact of the September 11th attacks on public consciousness and media coverage. In 2003, we see the prominence of keywords like 'Iraq', 'United States International Relations', and 'Housing', coinciding with the Iraq War and the focus on real estate that foreshadowed the coming housing market crisis. In 2008, 'Barack Obama' and 'United States Economy' surge in frequency, corresponding to Obama's election campaign and the global financial crisis. This visualization not only highlights the dynamic nature of news topics but also reflects historical events and shifts in public interest over time.

Current events shape our news landscape - How do topical news events shift the tone of articles across different sectors of news?

The visualization above, called a Line Chart, illustrates the average sentiment across various sections over time. We utilized sentiment analysis to gauge the emotional tone of the articles in each section, presenting a clear picture of how the mood of news reporting evolves. This visualization offers a deeper understanding of how different sections of news might respond similarly or differently to the same event. For example, the September 11 attacks in 2001 and the financial crisis of 2008 led to significant shifts in sentiment across all sections, highlighting how major events impact the overall tone of news reporting. It's important to note that some sections, such as Opinion and International News, may exhibit more extreme sentiment fluctuations compared to others. Users can interact with the visualization to explore how sentiment trends in different sections have evolved, reflecting the diverse ways in which The New York Times covers significant events.

Conclusion

Our visualizations have provided valuable insights into the publishing trends, keyword growth, and sentiment dynamics of New York Times articles over the years. By exploring the data through these visualizations, we can better understand how The New York Times has responded to historical events, how public interests have shifted, and how the tone of news reporting has evolved. We hope these visualizations inspire further exploration and analysis of media trends, contributing to a deeper understanding of the factors that shape the news landscape.

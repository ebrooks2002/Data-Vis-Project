Investigating Trends In The New York Times's Articles

Ethan Brooks and Tamur Asar

The following visualizations were constructed using a collection of over two million New York Times articles. The visualizations aim to illustrate common pattens and trends, be that cultural or semantic, that subliminaly exist and/or are intentionally constructed by The New York Times. While we do not have comprehensive datasets for other news agencies to compare to, we aim to extrapolate our findings as an explanation to broader trends that morph news during different time periods.

This visualization once again utilized our sentiment classifying tool to perform an analysis on the percentage of news articles released in a given year that can be classified as positive, neutral or negative. These calculations are then visualized and stacked in this bar chart for comparative analysis. Immediately you can recognize a trend in some of the bars. Perhaps most insightful is the the category for "World". Between 2000 and 2010, the category for World never has an overall negative sentiment quantifier under fifty percent. That means, year over year, over fifty percent of world news articles published by The New York Times will be focusing on a negative subject. We can contrast this to a category such as "Travel" where the category is overwhelmingly positive. This makes sense: travel news is typically functioning partially as an advertizement to emphasize the beauty of the world. In the travel section you will typically see great travel deals or trips people have taken. In contrast, the most commonly talked about world news subjects are international conflicts or drama. At the time of writing, opening The New York Times's World News page greets me with the Israel-Palestine conflict, Russia-Ukraine, War in the Congo, Chile's political revolution, a religious Cardinal on trial, etc. There are no positive world articles. Is that to say there is no positive world news? Or that The New York times gives what consumers want, and people are seeking out news about conflicts and not their resolutions.
